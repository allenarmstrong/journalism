Who Are Journalists?

There are many different ideas about what a journalist actually is. So how do you know the difference between a journalist and a nonjournalist?

There is one basic difference that is a perfect starting point: Journalists inform people about what is going on around them.

Beyond that, the definition becomes more difficult to pin down.

The government requires licenses for many professions to make sure people know how to do what they say they can do.

Examples:
  • A doctor must obtain licensing to practice medicine.
  • A lawyer has to pass the state bar exam.
  • A hairstylist takes a licensing exam.

However, the government does not license journalists.

Although there are many professional journalism schools that help students improve their chances to get hired, journalism school is not required by law. In effect, anyone who wants to can become a journalist.

Types of Journalists

Journalists in Television

Many people imagine traditional news anchors when they think of journalists. These journalists work for companies that often have established fact-checking rules and codes of ethics that each member of the news staff must follow.

Today, news anchors report on the news, as well as interview on-the-scene reporters about events. Often, they make their stories appropriate for both television and online audiences. They have full-time jobs with their stations and often remain journalists throughout their entire careers.


Things to Consider

  • Would you consider the members of an ENG team to be journalists, as well? ENG stands for electronic news gathering, and these people use digital cameras or satellites to gather video of events.
  • What about other members of the television staff, such as the executive producers and news assistants? Are they journalists?
 

Journalists in Print

Other people imagine traditional newspaper reporters when they think of journalists.

These journalists work for companies that normally have established fact-checking rules and codes of ethics that each member of the news staff must follow. Today’s newspaper reporters seek out, research, and report on the news, often making their stories appropriate for the printed newspaper, as well as for newspaper-run television stations and online websites. They have full-time jobs with their newspapers and often remain journalists throughout their entire careers.


Things to Consider

  • Would you consider the editors of a newspaper to be journalists, as well? These are the people who assign stories to reporters, do some of the fact-checking, and determine where the stories should appear within the newspaper.
  • What about other members of the newspaper staff, such as the news assistants and publishers? Are they journalists?
 

Journalists in Radio

Other people might imagine traditional radio broadcasters when they think of journalists. These journalists work for companies that usually have established fact-checking rules and codes of ethics that each member of the news staff must follow.

Today’s radio broadcasters report news to broadcast on the airways, as well as to stream on the Internet. They have full-time jobs with their stations and often remain journalists throughout their entire careers.

Things to Consider

  • Would you consider radio news directors to be journalists, as well? These are the people who manage the news staff. They pay attention to stories that come in on the wire, and they make decisions about what news to cover on the air.
  • What about other members of the radio staff, such as the producers and on-air hosts? Are they journalists?
