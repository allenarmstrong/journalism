Ethics in Feature Writing

Look at the Pulitzer Prize-winning story by Rick Bragg again. What makes this story work as a feature piece about American lives?

Gives an in-depth look at a story over time

Tells about the people and events – more like a narrative

Provides colorful details that allow the reader to really imagine what happened

Longer than a traditional news story

Answers the 5Ws and one H

Informs the public about an interesting topic

Required the journalist to do research and interview people

But what about ethics? Did Rick Bragg face any ethical concerns when he was writing this feature story?

For example, imagine he discovered personal details about Osceola McCarty and the Bullock family that they didn’t want to see printed in the newspaper. What should he have done in that situation?

Often, especially in feature writing, reporters will uncover strange or interesting facts. Some of these facts might be damaging to the people they’re writing about. A journalist must work with his or her organization to decide what belongs in the story and what doesn’t.

Remember, the Society of Professional Journalists recommends that you Seek Truth and Report It, Minimize Harm, Act Independently, and Be Accountable and Transparent.

Poynter also lists 10 useful questions you can ask yourself in order to make good ethical decisions. One of the most important to keep in mind is this:

Can I clearly and fully justify my thinking and my decision?

When you consider these important reminders before you write your story, you are much more likely to act ethically.
