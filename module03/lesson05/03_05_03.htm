Revising

Let’s start with revision. There are several things you should examine in your first draft to create the best final draft possible:

Do all examples of research, interview quotations, and descriptive anecdotes relate to your main topic?

Is the lead interesting and engaging?

Does the story include a clear "human interest" element to keep readers going?

Does the story have a clear beginning, middle, and end?

Are temporal words or phrases used to transition writing from one idea to the next?

Does the story move from beginning to end in a logical order?

Are vivid descriptions used to add interest to your story?

Do the tone and mood fit the topic selected?

Are the tone and mood consistent throughout the story? *Tip: Read the draft aloud to check for sentence fluency. Any parts that sound awkward should be revised.

Are the sentences varied in length and structure?

Have you used "action" verbs?

Can any short sentences be improved by combining them?

Can any long sentences be made more concise?

Are relevant and interesting lines from your interviews incorporated in your story?

Have you removed unnecessary adjectives and adverbs from your sentences?

Editing

Even the smallest errors—such as typos or grammatical problems—can harm your credibility as a journalist. People are much more willing to trust you when your writing isn’t filled with mistakes!

Once you have revised your writing, it is time to edit. Look for errors in grammar, structure, and punctuation.
