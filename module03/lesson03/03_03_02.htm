Touching Up Ethics

In 2003, photojournalist Brian Walski combined two images for the Los Angeles Times to make a more exciting picture. The change left readers believing a soldier had pointed his weapon at a man carrying a child, when the reality had been a bit different.

The picture ran in the Los Angeles Times, and other news organizations picked it up before people noticed the problem: it didn’t depict what actually happened. Walski had not been honest with his audience.

That dishonesty got Walski fired.

When people read, watch, or listen to the news, they’re looking for the truth. Altering a photograph for dramatic effect may make the picture more exciting, but it doesn’t make it true. Many news organizations and news consumers will get upset about that.

For your own feature news story, you will include images and/or sounds that will help tell your story. However, you must pay close attention to your own ethical behavior as you take pictures, record sounds, and prepare media to go with your writing.

Be honest!
