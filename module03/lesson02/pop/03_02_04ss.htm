Sentence Structure

In addition to using a good vocabulary when you write, another important part of writing and reading involves being able to understand sentence structure.

A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. A sentence is made up of a subject and a verb.

In our writing, we can use different structures for variety. We also need to avoid certain structures to keep our meaning clear. Select each of the following structures to learn more.

Independent Clauses

Independent clauses are full thoughts that do not need anything else in order to convey a message. Examples:

  • I went to the beach.
  • The author uses metaphors.
  • Summer is my favorite season.

Dependent Clauses

A dependent clause is a sentence fragment because it includes a subject and a verb but does not express a complete thought. The thought is left unfinished. It cannot stand on its own.

When I read the book.

Here are some other examples of dependent clauses that need other words added to make sense.

After I went to the beach.

Though I was looking forward to my favorite season, summer.

Sentence Fragments

A sentence fragment is a group of words that does not express a complete thought. It is missing either the subject, the verb, or both the subject and verb. Examples:

  • Fragment: Such as comic books, magazine articles, and films.
    Correction: Spiderman has been adapted to many different areas such as comic books, magazine articles, and films.
  • Fragment: Because I was hungry.
    Correction: I went to the store because I was hungry.
  • Fragment: Walking for hours.
    Correct: Walking for hours increases the heart rate and burns fat.
Sentence fragments may be used occasionally in fiction to indicate an abrupt thought or to alter the pace. However, they are considered a grammatical error. You should avoid using sentence fragments. To check whether you are using a fragment, try reading your sentences aloud. Ask yourself whether each sentence makes sense on its own.
