What's Already Been Said?

Chances are you won’t be the first person to write about your topic. That’s okay! However, you do want to find out what other people have said and what important information is out there.

There are three good reasons to do thorough research for your editorial:
  • The more you know about your topic, the better you can support your claim.
  • When you research opposing viewpoints, you’ll know exactly what to say about the counterclaim.
  • Strong evidence from credible sources will help you create a rebuttal that people will believe.

Use this checklist to guide your research:

I have searched online for articles or videos about my topic.

I have contacted experts on both sides of the issue about my topic to ask them for more information.

I have searched through archived information (online or at a library) to find a brief history of this topic.

I have found primary source information about my topic. This may include speeches, official reports, government documents, and statistics.

I have double-checked all of my sources to be sure they are reliable and trustworthy.

Remember: the more you know, the stronger your editorial will be!

5Ws and One H

Journalists live by the 5Ws and one H: Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How. These are important in just about every form of journalism you can write, including opinion pieces.

As you do your research, search for answers to those questions. Here are some ways you can approach the 5Ws and one H in your editorial research:


Who

Who agrees with this topic?

Who disagrees with this topic?

Who is affected by this topic?

… what other “Who” questions can you think of for your topic?

What

What is this history of this topic?

What are the most common claims on each side of the debate?

What are the stakes? In other words, what will happen if people don’t do what you think is right?

… what other “What” questions can you think of for your topic?

When

When did this debate begin?

When did the most important events in this debate take place?

When must people make up their minds?

… what other “When” questions can you think of for your topic?

Where

Where are people most affected by this topic?

Where can people go to get involved or learn more?

Where have the most important discussions been held?

… what other “Where” questions can you think of for your topic?

Why

Why should anybody care about this topic?

Why haven’t people come to an agreement already?

Why is your claim correct?

… what other “Why” questions can you think of for your topic?

How

How did this debate begin?

How have people been affected by this topic?

How can the issue be resolved?

… what other “How” questions can you think of for your topic?
