Pathos

When you think of pathos, think of the word “sympathy.” Writers use pathos to appeal to their audiences’ emotions and get them to believe their side of an argument.

Feature writers, editorial columnists, review writers and critics, and other opinion-based journalists use this method as part of their daily work.

A writer can use pathos in many ways. The most common is to select words that have emotional connotations associated with them. For instance, if you were to write about the act of hunting, you could choose one of the following sentences with emotionally charged words:

Hunting is essentially the murderous act of stomping into an animal’s home uninvited and destroying whatever you see with man-made acts of violence.
Hunting requires knowledge of the appropriate tools needed to kill and field dress animals while you are out in the woods.

NOTE

Because words like "you," "is," and "a" are simple parts of speech, they are generally considered neutral words with no strong connotations.

Word choices make a big difference in how information comes across to audiences. In addition to connotative choices, writers can manipulate the way they present subject matter so that it conjures up emotions in audiences.
