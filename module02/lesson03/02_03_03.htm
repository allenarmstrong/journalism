Pronouns

Pronouns must match their appropriate place in the sentence. What does this mean? Some pronouns will always take the place of the subject, and other pronouns will always take the place of the object.

What Are Pronouns, Subjects, and Objects?

Pronouns

Pronouns are the words we use to replace nouns. For instance, if your name is Maria, it would be uncommon to say, “Maria wants some cookies.” Instead, you’ll probably say, “I want some cookies.” In this situation, “I” is a pronoun. It takes the place of the subject, Maria.

Subjects and Objects

Subjects are the main idea of the sentence. They are the people or things performing the action. Objects receive the action of the sentence.

Here is an example:

Jill gave some cookies to Maria.

Pronouns can get a bit tricky when there are multiple subjects and objects in a sentence.

Colons and Semicolons

What’s the difference between a colon and a semicolon? There are some easy tricks for remembering these.

Colons

Colons look like the top of a smiley face emoticon. They must appear at the end of a full thought, and they often introduce a list. You can imagine the colon means “and here it is” or “and here they are.”

Here is an example:

When I was a kid, I wanted to be one of three things: a football quarterback, a ballet dancer, or a painter.

Semicolons

Semicolons look like the top of a winking face emoticon. They must appear in between two full thoughts that are related. Think of this punctuation mark as the sneaky one that allows you to write two sentences together without stopping—and—not get in trouble for using a run-on sentence!

Here is an example:

Sometimes your plans change; when I grew up, I decided to become a writer instead.

IMPORTANT REMINDER! To use a semicolon, your two full thoughts must be related. For example, you can’t write, “I became a writer; my dog likes cheese.”
