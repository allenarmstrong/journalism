Fortunes in Journalism

In the late 1800s and early 1900s, people had developed such an interest in news that it became possible to make a huge fortune as a publisher or editor in the journalism industry. One notable person to do this was William Randolph Hearst.

William Randolph Hearst made his fortune through sensationalizing journalism. He took over several publications and showed more of an interest in sales than in credibility. Many of his newspapers exploited stories to make them seem more sensational than they were in reality. This practice led to what became known as
yellow journalism.

In 1941, actor and director Orson Welles released a fictional exposé of the publishing industry, Citizen Kane. The film depicted a publisher much like William Randolph Hearst.

By the 1950s, many homes in the United States had at least one television, and the demand for regular news updates grew. In the 1980s, media tycoon Ted Turner invested in Cable News Network, or CNN. This became the first channel to provide 24-hour news coverage.

Billionaire Rupert Murdoch became a household name as he built his media empire through the acquisition of several companies. As CEO of News Corporation, Murdoch owns Fox News, the New York Post newspaper, HarperCollins Publishers, the 20th Century Fox film and TV studios, MySpace, and many other media sources.

Who Are the Gatekeepers Now?

With the rapid advancement of technology, more and more people are able to participate in the spreading of news. Suddenly it’s not only the trained and educated journalists who provide information; now anyone with an online connection can let the world know what is happening in his or her area in just moments.

In many ways, this is an exciting time because it means news can spread faster and more easily than ever before. However, it is also a time of rampant misinformation because we often don’t know which sources we can trust. In a world where anyone can post news online, who will make sure the “facts” are true?

As you have seen in this lesson, advances in technology have led to many changes in modern American journalism:

  • Photojournalism provides still images of important events that can shape public opinion.
  • Print journalism provides written news stories that people can read on their own.
  • Radio provides audio samples of news stories that people can listen to.
  • Television provides visual clips of news events that people can watch.
  • The Internet provides mobile versions of all news media that people can access anywhere.
