Grammar for Journalists

  1. Check for Subject/Verb Agreement
    Identify your subject as a singular or plural and use the corresponding verb.
    Singular: The officer wants to question the subject.
    Plural: The offers want to question he suspect.
  1. Be Sure to Use Correct Pronouns
    Subject pronouns do the action.
    I, we, you, he, she, it, they
    Object pronouns receive the action.
    Me, us, you, him, her, it, them
  1. Use Active Structure
    Active structure makes writing clear and concise.
    Active: Smith hit a home run, winning the game.
    Passive: The game was won after a home run was hit by Smith.
  1. Check For Parallel Structure
    Parallel structure gives equal meaning to words and clauses that appear in lists.
    Incorrect: The journalist used the dictionary in order to check spelling, find meaning, and looking up pronunciations.
    Correct: The journalist used the dictionary to check the spelling, find meaning, and look up pronunciations.
  1. Know How to Use a Colon
    Use when introducing a list or quotation.
    The following items were on the table: a rope, a candlestick, a revolver, and a lead pipe.
    Ms. Peacock’s remarks on her whereabouts were brief: “I was in the library.”

    Use between two simple sentences to emphasize the second.
    I don’t know why everyone likes that movie: the plot is so predictable.
  1. Know How to Use a Semicolon
    Use when linking simple sentences that are related.
    Jack likes tea; Jill prefers coffee.

    Use when items in a series already have commas.
    This summer I traveled to Anaheim, California; Denver, Colorado; and Nashville, Tennessee.
  1. Vary Your Phrasing for Interest
    Repetitive writing can bore readers. Use words and phrases that make writing come to life.
    Boring: On Friday the high school band performed. They marched onto the football field. They played the fight song. The crowd cheered. They wore blue and gold uniforms.
    Interesting: On Friday, a team of students in blue and gold uniforms marched onto the school field to the sounds of a cheering crowd. They weren’t there to play football. They were there to lead the pep rally.
  1. Always Check for Correct Spelling
    Spell check is not enough! Be sure to check for these commonly confused words:
    • accept/except
    • desert/dessert
    • it’s/its
    • loose/lose
    • principal/principle
    • their/there/they’re
    • to/too/two
