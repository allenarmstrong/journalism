Research

As you search for details about your chosen career, you may find conflicting information.This is a problem journalists deal with every day. Researching the validity of something can be a tricky process.
Take a look at this research tutorial for a similar situation.

When you research, you search for information. Journalists must take this process seriously in order to protect their credibility. A joke among journalists is, “If your mother tells you she loves you, check the source.” This is obviously meant to be funny, but the joke hints at a journalistic quandary. When you get information from people you don’t know or trust, how can you verify what they tell you is true?

How can you verify what is true?

Check other sources. Find someone who is a known expert, either because the person is a professional in the field or because the person has experienced the topic firsthand. Interview the expert to get his or her thoughts. Find others you can talk to, as well.

Getting Started

Most Internet searches begin with a search engine such as Google or Bing.

Once you’re there, type in your search. For example, you might type "Reporting careers in television journalism." Be as specific as possible to narrow down the results. For example, typing in "Journalism careers" is going to give you very different results than "Reporting careers in television journalism."

Evaluating Results

Even when you use a specific search string (in this example "Reporting careers in television journalism"), you are still going to get a lot of results. Now you need to decide which ones are best for your research. Think about the types of sources you’ve found. Which ones will best fit your research needs?

Authority

Overview: Does the source have authority? If so, other expert sources will agree with the information.

Questions to Ask: Trusted source? Qualified author?

Bias

Overview: Is it biased? If so, the writer’s opinions will be slanted one way or the other and will not be objective.

Questions to Ask: Objective information? Tries to persuade readers?

Accuracy

Overview: It is accurate? If so, you will find several other sources that would agree.

Questions to Ask: Agrees with other sources? Cites other sources?

Currency

Overview: How current is the information? Some industries, like medicine and technology, are constantly coming up with new advancements. Using information more than five years old will likely be outdated. However, some studies, like language and history, are not likely to change as quickly.

Questions to Ask: Up to date? When was it published?

Always Keep in Mind...

Reliable sources are accurate, authoritative, unbiased, and current.

Which person would be more likely to have a bias toward a specific organization?

  • the person who owns the organization and makes a lot of money from it
  • the outside person who has compared the organization with others like it

Which article would be more accurate?

  • an article that uses other sources to support the findings
  • an article that goes against everything else you have found in your research

Remember to be careful when you search the Internet. You should never give away your personal information or visit a site that you feel is inappropriate.
